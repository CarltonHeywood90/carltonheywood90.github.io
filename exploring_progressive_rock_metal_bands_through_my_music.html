Progressive rock metal isn’t just heavy riffs or technical prowess—it’s about exploring unconventional song structures, unusual rhythms, and rich textures. Bands like TesseracT, Porcupine Tree, and A Perfect Circle take the standard rock or metal formula and push it into unexpected territories, blending intensity with inventiveness.

For me, these bands have shaped how I approach songwriting and performance. I don’t just admire them—I try to channel their spirit into my own music, creating something that feels complex, layered, and personal.

What makes a band “progressive rock metal”?

Progressive rock metal challenges both the listener and the musician. It’s about evolving song structures, polyrhythms, odd meters, and atmospheric textures. It’s not about virtuosity for its own sake—it’s about emotion, tension, and surprise.

My favorite techniques and riffs

One of my tracks, “Expectation and Reality” on SoundCloud, draws heavily from TesseracT. It combines triplet-based arpeggios over a straight 5/8 bass and drum groove, with the snare hitting 2 and 4 in a polyrhythm and truncating every eighth bar of 4/4.

Another track, “Sowing Thorns”, reflects Porcupine Tree influences: sus2 chords, a 7/8 time signature, and vocal melodies reminiscent of Steven Wilson.

Translating influences into my music

I absorb the techniques of these bands—polyrhythms, arpeggiated textures, suspended chords—and reinterpret them through my own sensibilities. The goal is not imitation, but crafting music that feels authentic, intricate, and emotionally honest.

Behind-the-scenes of my latest track

Creating progressive-inspired music starts with experimenting with rhythm and harmony. In “Expectation and Reality”, I layered guitars, bass, and drums to make the odd meters feel natural and expressive rather than mechanical.
